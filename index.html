Storygrounds: Place-based reading

Storygrounds

Reading a novel through the ground it stands on.

Every one of these books happens somewhere. A family is driven off a farm and pointed down a numbered highway. A boy and a man drift south on a river that is carrying them the wrong way. Put those places on a map and the story stops being background and starts being evidence — something students can trace, measure, and argue with.

Each study below pairs an interactive map with the text, and with a bank of questions built on three levels.

1

What's where?

Location, description, and spatial vocabulary. Students read the map itself before they interpret anything.

2

Why is it there?

The processes that put things in their places — rivers, rainfall, roads, borders, money, and power.

3

What's the relationship to me?

Comparison, connection, and personal geography. The step where a map becomes an argument about the student's own world.

About this project

Storygrounds is built for the American literature classroom. Each study can be read straight through, scrolled like a story, or projected for a class — hide the text and the map takes the whole screen, with arrow keys to step through the stops.

Teachers can check the questions they want from any of the three levels and copy them out for a handout, a discussion guide, or an exit ticket.

The three-level framework follows the geographic inquiry tradition of what is where, why there, and why care?
