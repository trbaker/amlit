Storygrounds: Place-based reading

One Mile Downstream

Mapping Chris McCandless's road in Jon Krakauer's Into the Wild (1996)

In May 1990 a young man graduated from Emory University with honors, gave away the twenty-four thousand dollars in his savings account, told his family nothing, and drove west. Two years later a moose hunter found his body in an abandoned bus on a trail in interior Alaska. He was twenty-four years old.

This study is not an argument about whether he was brave or foolish — people have been having that argument for thirty years, and students should be allowed to have it too. It is an attempt to put his two years on a map, because the map turns out to be the story.

On July 5, 1992, he tried to walk out. A river stopped him and he went back to the bus. Less than a mile downstream from where he stood, strung across that same river, was a hand-operated cable car that would have carried him to the other side. He had no map that showed it. He never knew it was there.

For the Teacher

Three Levels of Geographic Questioning

A note before you begin. Chris McCandless was a real person who died, and his family is still living. The questions below are written to let students argue honestly about his choices without treating his death as a punchline or a parable. The most useful thing this map can do is replace opinion with distance: how far, how deep, how cold, how close.

Each stop below carries one question from each level, and the banks here hold more. Check the questions you want, then copy your selection for a handout, discussion guide, or exit ticket. The sequence moves students from location to explanation to meaning — in the spirit of the geographer's ladder: what is where, why there, and why care?

1 What's where?

Location, description, and spatial vocabulary — reading the map itself.

2 Why is it there?

Spatial reasoning — the processes that put things in their places.

3 What's the larger relationship — and the relationship to me?

Connection, comparison, and personal geography — why care?

After the Map

What the Map Would Have Told Him

That the river runs highest in July. That a gauging station sits a mile downstream with a cable and a basket. That the highway is twenty miles off. That there are cabins within a walk of the bus. None of this makes him a fool — plenty of experienced people have died in that country — and none of it makes the book less worth reading. It simply means that the thing standing between him and the rest of his life was information, and information about places is what a map is.

That is worth sitting with, in a course where students are being asked to read literature with maps in their hands.

Teacher notes — read before assigning

Chris McCandless was a real person who died in 1992 at twenty-four, and members of his family are living. The material here is written to let students judge his choices honestly without turning his death into a joke or a moral lesson with a tidy answer. Krakauer's own book is a defense of him against exactly that. The cause of death is not fully settled. Starvation is the consensus; Krakauer has argued over the years that a toxin in wild potato seeds contributed, and that argument remains contested. Students should be told it is contested rather than handed one version. Locations are approximate. The bus site is well documented; the exact points where he attempted the July crossing and where the gauging station stood are placed here from Krakauer's descriptions and are best treated as "about here" rather than surveyed. The hand tram is described in the book as being less than a mile downstream of his crossing. Bus 142 was lifted out by helicopter by the Alaska Army National Guard in June 2020, after years of hikers being injured and at least two drowning in the Teklanika while trying to reach it. It was moved to the University of Alaska Museum of the North in Fairbanks. Anyone in your class who starts talking about going to see it should be told this, and told about the river. The author is Jon Krakauer. The three-level questioning framework follows the geographic inquiry tradition of "What is where? Why there? Why care?"
