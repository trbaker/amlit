Storygrounds: Place-based reading

The Road West

Mapping the Joad family's journey in John Steinbeck's The Grapes of Wrath (1939)

In the spring of an unnamed Depression year, the Joad family is tractored off their farm near Sallisaw, Oklahoma. They load three generations onto a used Hudson truck and join thousands of other families on U.S. Route 66, bound for the promise of work in California — roughly 1,650 miles away.

For the Teacher

Three Levels of Geographic Questioning

Each stop below carries one question from each level, and the banks here hold more. Check the questions you want, then copy your selection for a handout, discussion guide, or exit ticket. The sequence moves students from location to explanation to meaning — in the spirit of the geographer's ladder: what is where, why there, and why care?

1 What's where?

Location, description, and spatial vocabulary — reading the map itself.

2 Why is it there?

Spatial reasoning — the processes that put things in their places.

3 What's the larger relationship — and the relationship to me?

Connection, comparison, and personal geography — why care?

After the Map

Where the Road Ends

The novel closes not with arrival but with flood — the family has traveled sixteen hundred miles to find the same precariousness they fled, and the last act of the book is a gift from one stranger to another. Geography got the Joads to California; it could not, by itself, get them a home. That gap between place and belonging is a fine place for a class discussion to end — or to begin.

Teacher notes

The route and the mileages are generalized from the novel's references to U.S. 66, and are approximate. Sallisaw, Bethany, Santa Rosa, Needles, Daggett, Bakersfield, and the Weedpatch camp are real places named or clearly implied in the novel. The peach ranch, the boxcar camp, and the final barn are Steinbeck's inventions, placed here at their approximate setting in the Tulare basin. Steinbeck never dates the novel precisely; it is set in the mid-to-late 1930s. The three-level questioning framework follows the geographic inquiry tradition of "What is where? Why there? Why care?"
