Beneath the Ground

Mapping Cora's flight in Colson Whitehead's The Underground Railroad (2016)

The real Underground Railroad was a metaphor: a network of people, safe houses, footpaths, and lies. Whitehead takes the metaphor and buries it. In this novel there is an actual railroad under the actual ground — engines, tracks, timetables, station agents with lanterns — running beneath a country that would kill you for standing on top of it.

Cora runs from a Georgia cotton plantation and surfaces in one state after another. Each one looks, at first, like a rescue. Each one is a different way of destroying her. The train does not take her to freedom; it takes her through a catalogue of America.

For the Teacher

Three Levels of Geographic Questioning

A note on this map. The boundaries shown are from 1854, not from today. There are no modern highways, no modern city labels, and no modern state lines — only terrain and the borders as they stood at the time. States are shaded by whether slavery was legal in them; the western territories, still undecided, are left unshaded and outlined in dashes. Several states on a present-day map do not exist yet. Tell students this before they begin, and then ask them what it changes.

The shading is the whole book in one image: Cora is trying to get from the red into the blue, and the train keeps surfacing in the red.

Each stop below carries one question from each level, and the banks here hold more. Check the questions you want, then copy your selection for a handout, discussion guide, or exit ticket. The sequence moves students from location to explanation to meaning — in the spirit of the geographer's ladder: what is where, why there, and why care?

1 What's where?

Location, description, and spatial vocabulary — reading the map itself.

2 Why is it there?

Spatial reasoning — the processes that put things in their places.

3 What's the larger relationship — and the relationship to me?

Connection, comparison, and personal geography — why care?

After the Map

Where the Line Ends

It doesn't. Cora pumps a handcar down a tunnel nobody built to anywhere, comes up through a hole in the ground, and takes a seat on a wagon going west with a driver she has just met. That is the whole ending. The railroad that was supposed to deliver her has no terminus, because the country on the surface never built one.

Ask a class to lay this map beside the other two in this collection. The Joads travel west and the map at least promises something. Huck and Jim travel south and the map betrays them. Cora travels through a map with a trapdoor in every state. Three journeys, one country.

Teacher notes — read before assigning

This is not a historical novel, and the map is not a historical map. Whitehead writes speculative history: the literal railroad is his invention, and the states Cora passes through are deliberate anachronisms. South Carolina's benevolent doctors performing forced sterilizations and running a secret syphilis study compress the real 20th-century Tuskegee study and decades of coerced sterilization. North Carolina's total ban on Black people, enforced by a road of hanged bodies, gathers together lynching, sundown towns, and expulsion. Indiana's destroyed Black farming community echoes Tulsa, Rosewood, and Wilmington. Students should be told this plainly, and then asked to go find the real events and the real places. The Randall plantation, Griffin, the Valentine farm, and the stations are inventions. They are placed here in the regions the novel implies — Georgia's cotton belt, the South Carolina midlands, the North Carolina piedmont, middle Tennessee, and southern Indiana. Distances and station locations are approximate. The base of this map is terrain only — no modern roads, labels, or state lines, which would show learners a country that did not yet exist. The political boundaries are the 1854 states and territories. The shading is not part of that source data: it is applied here, classifying each state by whether slavery was legal in it in 1854. The territories are left unshaded because in 1854 they were precisely the open question. Note that the novel is set loosely in this period rather than in a fixed year, and that Whitehead's states are inventions in any case — the map shows the real country the story is arguing with. The real Underground Railroad kept almost no records and drew almost no maps, for the obvious reason. What is mapped today is reconstructed from testimony, court records, and the memoirs of the people who survived it. The three-level questioning framework follows the geographic inquiry tradition of "What is where? Why there? Why care?"
